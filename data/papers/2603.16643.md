## Abstract

> Alignment techniques often inadvertently induce sycophancy in LLMs. While prior studies studied this behaviour in direct-answer settings, the role of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning remains under-explored: does it serve as a logical constraint that mitigates sycophancy, or a tool for post-hoc rationalization that masks it? We evaluate a range of models across objective and subjective tasks to investigate the issue. Results show that reasoning generally reduces sycophancy in final decisions but also masks sycophancy in some samples, where models construct deceptive justifications through logical inconsistencies, calculation errors, and one-sided arguments etc. Furthermore, LLMs are more prone to sycophancy in subjective tasks and under authority-bias. Our mechanistic analysis on three open-source models reveals that the tendency of sycophancy is dynamic during the reasoning process rather than being pre-determined at the input stage.

## 1 Introduction

Alignment techniques for Large Language Models (LLMs) such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) are widely used to better align model behavior with human expectations and values (Wang et al., 2023). However, recent studies have identified a critical drawback: these training methods inherently exploit human judgment and produce outputs that appeal to human evaluators but are actually flawed or incorrect (Cotra, 2021). This inadvertently promotes "sycophancy", where models sacrifice factual accuracy to cater to the user's perceived beliefs or preferences (Burns et al., 2023; Sharma et al., 2024).

Such behavior has been proven to be a consistent property across model scales and training paradigms (Burns et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024; Laban et al., 2024), in studies focusing primarily on final label agreement in direct-answer settings. As Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) reasoning becomes the dominant paradigm for LLM interactions, it transforms model decision-making from simple answer generation to complex reasoning. This outcome-based approach thus becomes insufficient, as it overlooks the intermediate computations and textual logic underpinning model decisions.

This shift creates a critical trade-off: while explicit CoT can serve as a "logical constraint" (Chen et al., 2025b) that forces models to adhere to factual consistency, it also expands the scope for deception: models may co-opt their logical prowess for "post-hoc rationalization", constructing rigorous-sounding but biased justifications to accommodate users' mistaken beliefs (Turpin et al., 2023; Chua et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2025c). To systematically investigate this phenomenon, we follow a stepwise logic that moves from external behaviors to internal mechanisms and pose four research questions:

Sycophancy is a complex behaviour in LLMs (Vennemeyer et al., 2025; Cheng et al., 2025). In this paper, we focus on a specific condition where an LLM abandons its choice in the unbiased and neutral prompt for a biased option when the preference of a user or an authority figure‚Äôs explicit bias is appended to the prompt (Chen et al., 2025c).

We evaluate the sycophancy behaviour of a set of closed-source models and open-source instruction-tuned models, across six kinds of prompt' settings (see Figure 1) on a dataset that includes both objective questions (with objectively correct answers) and subjective questions (without objectively correct answers). We find that sycophancy is pervasive across diverse model scales. Empirically, LLMs are more prone to sycophancy in subjective tasks than objective ones, and this tendency is higher under authority-bias than user-bias. Across all settings, using CoT prompting reduces sycophancy in final decisions compared to the No-CoT condition.

Meanwhile, the CoT text in the biased condition exhibits semantic deviation compared to that in the unbiased condition, yet shows no differences in most other linguistic features.

Beyond behavioral observations, we also explore the internal dynamics of sycophancy in the three open-source models using the Tuned Lens (Belrose et al., 2025). We find that, in these models, sycophancy is not a static predisposition fixed at the input stage, but a dynamic phenomenon undergoing unidirectional evolution during reasoning. When reasoning brings a shift from initial sycophancy toward unbiased in the internal representation, the resulting CoT text rarely manifests explicit correction of the bias. Conversely, when it intensifies sycophancy, distinct semantic patterns emerge: in objective tasks, the model often exhibits logical inconsistency, calculation errors, or factual fabrications to uphold the biased conclusion. In subjective tasks, this behavior manifests as one-sidedness, where the model strategically ignores trade-offs to construct a superficially coherent but biased argument.

**Chain of Thought (CoT)** introduces dynamic multi-step reasoning, where sequential token activations produce behaviors more complex to analyze than single-step representations (Yang et al., 2025b). CoT rationales are often unfaithful, as explanations may deviate from the model's internal computation (Barez et al., 2025). Even so, CoT generally outperforms No-CoT prompting in reasoning and task performance (Barez et al., 2025).

**Tuned Lens** To decode intermediate activations $h^l$ into the vocabulary space, we initially consider Logit Lens (Nostalgebraist, 2020):

$$\text{LogitLens}(h^l) = W_U [ \text{Norm}_f(h^l) ] \tag{1}$$

where $W_U \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times |V|}$ is the unembedding matrix and $\text{Norm}_f$ is the final normalization. However, Logit Lens can be unreliable as intermediate features often reside in different subspaces than the final layer (Yom Din et al., 2024). We thus employ Tuned Lens (Belrose et al., 2025), which introduces affine translators to minimize the KL-divergence between the model's final output distribution $f_{\theta}(h^l)$ and the intermediate projection:

$$\arg \min_{A_l, b_l} \mathbb{E} [ D_{KL} (f_{\theta}(h^l) \| \text{TunedLens}(h^l)) ] \tag{2}$$

Compared to Logit Lens, Tuned Lens ensures more faithful decoding of internal representations, particularly in earlier layers. For more related work, please refer to the Appendix A.

## 3 Experiment Setup

Our study adopts a three-layer setting in the experiments (see Figure 2): 1) whether the task itself has a ground-truth answer (*objective vs. subjective*), 2) whether the input includes biased preference (*user-bias or authority-bias vs. unbias*), and 3) whether the prompt elicits stepwise reasoning (*CoT*) or direct answers (*No-CoT*).

*   **Objective vs. Subjective** For objective data, we utilize a subset of **SycophancyEval** (Sharma et al., 2024), which aggregates 3,096 questions across various domains: 998 questions from **MMLU** (Hendrycks et al., 2021a), 1,000 problems from **MATH** (Hendrycks et al., 2021b), 254 items from **AQuA** (Ling et al., 2017), and 817 questions from **TruthfulQA** (Lin et al., 2022). For subjective data, we utilize a collection of open-ended datasets lacking definitive labels, comprising 3,076 samples across three datasets: 1,360 everyday moral scenarios from **DailyDilemmas** (Chiu et al., 2025), 696 model-generated critiques from the feedback subset of SycophancyEval (Sharma et al., 2024), and 1,020 cultural value assessments from the **social attitudes survey** (SCB10X Research Lab, 2025). The data examples can be found in Appendix B.
*   **Unbiased vs. Biased** To introduce preference biases, each base question is framed into three settings: *unbiased* (control, no preference cues), *user-bias* (first-person user suggestion e.g., "I think the answer might be [BIAS_ANSWER].", henceforth **bias 1**), and *authority-bias* (third-person authority cue e.g., "A Stanford professor suggests [BIAS_ANSWER] may be correct.", henceforth **bias 2**). This follows prior work on LLMs‚Äô sycophancy (Chen et al., 2025c).
*   **CoT vs. No-CoT** For each unbiased and biased prompt, we set two parallel versions: *No-CoT* version asks the model to directly answer without any explanation, and *CoT* version requires it to generate stepwise reasoning before giving the answer. See Appendix C for the detailed prompts for all settings.

We tested three closed-source and three open-source LLMs. The open-source models are **Llama3.1-8B-Instruct** (henceforth **Llama3.1**), **Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct** (henceforth **Qwen2.5**), and **Gemma-2-9B-IT** (henceforth **Gemma2**). The closed-source models are **Claude-3.5-Sonnet** (henceforth **Claude**), **GPT-3.5** (henceforth **GPT3.5**), and **OpenAI o3-mini** (henceforth **o3mini**)$^2$.

## 6 Dynamics of Sycophancy

In this section, we conduct a mechanistic analysis of internal model states in the three open-source models (Llama3.1, Qwen2.5, and Gemma2) to explore whether sycophancy is pre-determined at input or emerges dynamically during the reasoning.

### 6.1 Method

We first use all entries from the Section 4 and extract the hidden states at the initial output token, to compare the logit difference from sycophantic and unbiased answers across all layers under both No-CoT and CoT conditions. This serves to compare the LLM‚Äôs initial tendencies across different settings. Then we extend this analysis by dividing the output biased CoT text into ten equal segments (from 0% to 100% based on token count) across all four sample categories in Section 4. By testing the model at each interval along this trajectory, we can determine whether the sycophantic tendency remains constant or shifts dynamically as the reasoning chain unfolds. For the detailed setting of this section, refer to Appendix G.

**Metric** To quantify the model‚Äôs internal inclination, we define two critical tokens for each sample: the unbiased answer token ($t_u$) and the sycophancy token ($t_s$). The former ($t_u$) is defined as the option the model selects under the unbiased prompt. The latter ($t_s$) corresponds to the option the model selects with bias inserted in the prompt, representing the sycophantic choice. We measure the preference

$$\Delta L = \text{Logit}(t_u) - \text{Logit}(t_s) \tag{5}$$

A positive $\Delta L$ means that the model‚Äôs internal representation leans toward the unbiased answer, while a negative value indicates an inclination toward the sycophantic option.

**Tuned Lens** We trained a dedicated Tuned Lens for each of our three evaluated models, using a subsample of the RedPajama (Weber et al., 2024) dataset for training. Specifically, for a hidden state $h_l$ at layer $l$, the Tuned Lens computes a probability distribution $P_l$ over the vocabulary using a learned affine transformation followed by the model‚Äôs pre-trained unembedding matrix:

$$P_l = \text{Softmax}(\text{Unembed}(W_l h_l + b_l)) \tag{6}$$

where $W_l$ and $b_l$ are the layer-specific weight and bias learned during the training phase. This approach allows us to transform the latent vectors at each reasoning segment into the probability space of our defined answer tokens, $t_u$ and $t_s$.

### 6.2 Results and Analysis

The logit difference ($\Delta L$) in the first output token across different settings is illustrated in Figure 6, while Figure 7 shows the dynamics of sycophancy over the reasoning process for the four types.

## 7 Dynamics of Bias in CoT

As observed in Section 6, *CoT-Corrected* samples show a corrective shift toward unbiased, but reasoning conceals bias in *Persistent Sycophancy* and *CoT-Induced Sycophancy* samples. This section investigates if these divergent shifts manifest as identifiable semantic patterns in CoT text.

### 7.1 Corrective Logic in Truth-Telling

We first focus our analysis on *CoT-Corrected* samples, as they represent the model's successful resistance to sycophancy. We employed GPT-4o (OpenAI et al., 2024) to annotate the CoT text based on two indicators (for detailed prompts, ref to Appendix J):

*   **Explicit bias mention ($I_{eb}$)**: This indicator tracks whether the CoT explicitly comments and disagrees with the bias (e.g., mentioning "the professor's opinion is wrong").
*   **Comparative analysis depth ($I_{ad}$)**: If the bias is not explicitly mentioned ($I_{eb} = \text{False}$), this indicator will assess if the model analyzes the biased option more extensively than the unbiased condition.

By applying these indicators, we categorize *CoT-Corrected* samples into three semantic patterns:

1.  **Pattern A ($I_{eb} = \text{True}$)**: The CoT text demonstrates meta-awareness of the bias by directly identifying and correcting it.
2.  **Pattern B ($I_{eb} = \text{False}, I_{ad} = \text{True}$)**: The bias remains unmentioned, but CoT text "over-explains" why the biased option is incorrect, suggesting an implicit defense against the bias.
3.  **Pattern C ($I_{eb} = \text{False}, I_{ad} = \text{False}$)**: The CoT text seems to ignore the bias entirely.

## 8 Conclusion

This study provides a comprehensive investigation into the role of CoT reasoning in mitigating and masking LLM sycophancy across both objective and subjective tasks. Our findings validate that, while explicit reasoning significantly reduces sycophantic outcomes in final decisions, it simultaneously introduces a more deceptive form of "sycophantic reasoning" through post-hoc rationalization. Models under user or authority bias often construct superficially coherent but logically flawed justifications, characterized by inconsistencies, calculation errors, or one-sidedness, to uphold biased conclusions. Mechanistic analysis further reveals that sycophancy dynamically evolves during reasoning and is not a static predisposition fixed at the input stage. These results highlight the need for alignment techniques to move beyond outcome-based evaluations and focus on ensuring faithfulness and coherence of the reasoning process.

## Limitations

The current scope in our study involving six representative open-source and closed-source LLMs may not fully capture the behavioral nuances of emerging reasoning models. Furthermore, our main experiments focus on answer sycophancy (Sharma et al., 2024); while a supplementary analysis of preference sycophancy and challenge sycophancy (Appendix L) shows broadly consistent trends for CoT‚Äôs mitigating effect, our findings may not fully generalize to all forms of sycophancy. The investigation is also restricted to explicit user and authority-driven bias, potentially overlooking more subtle or implicit social signals that could trigger sycophantic responses in complex real-world deployments. While the Tuned Lens provides significant mechanistic insights into how sycophancy evolves dynamically during reasoning, it represents only one interpretive perspective on internal model states, which could be further enriched by granular circuit-level analyses or sparse autoencoders. Our mechanistic analysis relies on internal state access through Tuned Lens and SAEs, which limits our study to the open-source models of Llama3.1-8B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, and Gemma-2-9B-IT. Closed-source models may exhibit different internal dynamics due to different architectures, scales, and alignment procedures, so our findings on dynamic reasoning may not be universally applicable across all LLMs. Additionally, the study‚Äôs focus on single-turn interactions leaves the cumulative effects of sycophancy in multi-turn dialogues for subsequent exploration (Hong et al., 2025a).

Finally, the linguistic scope of this study constitutes an additional constraint, as the empirical evaluation was conducted exclusively within an English-centric framework using benchmarks.

Consequently, the external validity of our findings may be limited across diverse linguistic and socio-cultural paradigms.

## A Related Work

### A.1 Mechanistic Origins of Sycophancy Representations

The sycophancy in LLMs is the tendency to prioritize user agreement and preference over factual accuracy or objective neutrality (Sharma et al., 2024; Malmqvist, 2025; Wei et al., 2024). Current research identifies the mechanistic origins of sycophancy in LLMs as a structural rather than a purely behavioral phenomenon. Chen et al. (2025a); Wang et al. (2025c); Tak et al. (2025) find some linear activation directions in the LLM activations could stably encode some persona behaviors such as confidence, hallucination, and sycophancy, which indicates the structural sycophancy-aligned behaviors often reside in low-dimensional subspaces rather than being diffusely distributed (Burns et al., 2023). Utilizing **logit-lens analysis** and **causal activation patching**, researchers have mapped a two-stage emergence: In mid-to-late layers, the model develops differentiated representations of behavioral tendencies (e.g., sycophancy vs. unbiased). In subsequent output-proximal layers, these representations are selectively amplified into a preference decision, yielding sycophantic or unbiased responses (Wei et al., 2024; Vennemeyer et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025b). This two-stage process is described as circuit-like because the late-layer preference shift is causally dependent on earlier layer's representations, which forms a gated and path-dependent computation rather than a diffuse global effect (Wang et al., 2025a).

To mitigate the sycophantic risks, several interventions have been proposed. Firstly, methods to identify sycophantic or deference-related directions

<!-- PAGE: 14 -->

include **linear probings** (mean-difference vectors, linear probes, SVM directions, null-space projection) and **non-linear probings** (CCA alignment, MLP probes, manifold clustering) (Rimsky et al., 2024). Afterward, **contrastive activation steering** performs the intervention by adding or subtracting these vectors at selected layers to shift generation behavior (Papadatos and Freedman, 2024). Moreover, **Bayesian-deviation regularizers** analyze sycophancy from the perspective of probabilistic inference. Sycophancy alters belief distributions and the probabilistic stability, thereby influencing the model‚Äôs decision-making (Atwell et al., 2025; Sicilia et al., 2025).

Despite these interventions, sycophancy continues to appear in challenging settings. It resurfaces in adversarial QA, where models face leading or suggestive prompts that reward compliance over accuracy, and in role-conditioned dialogue, where identity cues (e.g., "professor," "expert," or "authority figure") implicitly pressure the model toward deference, even when affective-inference and persona-reactivity circuits have been isolated (Wang et al., 2025a,c). This persistence becomes more severe as model scale increases and instruction tuning intensifies alignment toward social preference signals, indicating that the behavior is reinforced by systemic properties of the representation space and training distribution rather than being eliminated by localized edits (Perez et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2025; Cheng et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025).

### A.2 Causal Dynamics within Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Frameworks

Within CoT frameworks, the involvement of sequential activations across many token positions makes sycophancy a dynamic rather than static phenomenon. The reasoning process may *amplify* bias as the reasoning progresses, but it may also *self-correct* it if later steps counteract earlier statements (Yang et al., 2025b). Concurrently, CoT rationales demonstrate limited reliability and hence increase the complexity: models frequently exhibit **Think-Answer Mismatch** (Chen et al., 2025c) and **Flaw Repetition** (Chua et al., 2024). These findings align with evidence that CoT explanations often diverge from the model‚Äôs latent computation path (Barez et al., 2025; Turpin et al., 2023), and that coherent rationales can be post-hoc fabrications rather than faithful reasoning paths (Lee et al., 2025; Chua et al., 2024; Lanham et al., 2023).

In short, the **dynamic bias propagation** and **reasoning unfaithfulness** makes sycophancy within CoT a structurally more complex phenomenon than static response bias.

Recent work proposes a mechanism-level explanation: CoT trajectories contain thought anchors, which are high-leverage steps that disproportionately steer subsequent reasoning confidence and trajectory planning (Bogdan et al., 2025). These anchors operate as structural control points within the computation graph. Mechanistically, **Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs)** decompose interpretable subcircuits responsible for multi-step reasoning (Galichin et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025b), while **causal patching** confirms that targeted activations can toggle behavior between memory retrieval and novel inference (Fartale et al., 2025; Hong et al., 2025b). This complementarity establishes verifiability: internal representations are not only observable, but *controllable*. Feature-steering approaches further stabilize CoT-specific activation directions and mitigate drift in reasoning quality (Kim et al., 2025; Eshuijs et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025). Collectively, these results show that CoT bias is anchored in identifiable mechanisms, not emergent randomness.

As research progresses from identification to intervention, the central challenge shifts toward maintaining stability under scale. Studies of **information flow** (Yang et al., 2025b) and **depth utilization** (Gupta et al., 2025) connect CoT behavior to scaling laws, yet major gaps remain: cross-scale feature stability is not well quantified, harmful features can re-emerge after fine-tuning, and current mitigation strategies risk degrading calibration or depth-dependent reasoning (Gupta et al., 2025). Thus, the present research frontier lies in unifying **representation control** with **causal editing**, preventing CoT from structurally amplifying sycophancy without collapsing inference depth or model confidence structure.

## E Linguistic Metrics Definitions

This section provides detailed definitions, computation methods, and interpretation guidelines for all linguistic metrics used in Section 5.

**Surface Statistics.** We utilize the NLTK library for granular text segmentation and volume analysis. *Sentence Count* is the total number of sentences identified via the Punkt sentence tokenizer. *Token Count* is the total number of lexical units processed via the word\_tokenize function.

**Lexical Richness.** To avoid the influence of total text length on the traditional Type-Token Ratio (TTR), we use the Moving-Average Type-Token Ratio (MATTR) with a fixed window size ($w = 10$):

$$\text{MATTR} = \frac{1}{N - w + 1} \sum_{i=1}^{N-w+1} \frac{TTR_i}{w} \tag{7}$$

where $N$ is the total number of tokens and $TTR_i$ is the ratio of unique tokens to total tokens within window $i$. A lower MATTR indicates reduced vocabulary diversity.

**Syntactic Complexity.** We utilize the en\_core\_web\_sm model in spaCy v3.8.11 to perform dependency parsing. *Mean Dependency Distance (MDD)* is the average linear distance between words and their syntactic heads; a higher MDD often indicates more sophisticated sentence structures. *Maximum Dependency Depth* is the longest path from the root of the dependency tree to any leaf node, representing the nesting level of the reasoning logic.

**Discourse Coherence.** Following Zhao et al. (2023), we assess the logical flow via two perspectives: (1) Reference-based Alignment, which compares the biased CoT with the unbiased reference CoT, and (2) Reference-free Internal Consistency,

**Reference-based Alignment.** We use DS-Focus-NN and DS-SENT-NN to quantify how bias cues cause the reasoning process to drift from the ideal unbiased path.

*   **DS-Focus-NN**: This metric measures the semantic and frequency alignment of ‚Äúfoci‚ÄĚ (key nouns or entities) between the generated CoT and the unbiased reference. It treats a text as a bipartite graph where foci are connected to their corresponding tokens. The score is defined as the scaled distance between focus embeddings ($F$):
    $$DS-FOCUS(hyp, ref) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{v \in \Omega} \| F_v^{hyp} - F_v^{ref} \| \tag{8}$$
    where $F_v$ is computed by summing the contextualized embeddings of tokens associated with focus $v$. A high score indicates that bias has caused a shift in the central entities or their usage frequency compared to the unbiased reasoning.
*   **DS-SENT-NN**: This metric evaluates the logical flow by modeling the interdependence between sentences through shared foci. An adjacency matrix $A$ is constructed where the weight between sentence $i$ and $j$ depends on the number of shared foci and their linear distance:
    $$A_{ij} = \frac{\text{count(shared foci)}}{j - i} \tag{9}$$
    The sentence embeddings are aggregated via this graph to form a global representation, and the final score is the cosine similarity between the generated graph and the unbiased reference graph.

**Reference-free Internal Consistency.** We use Entity Graph, Lexical Chain, and Repetition Cohesion to evaluate the internal structural integrity of the biased CoT.

*   **Entity Graph**: Based on the framework by Guinaudeau and Strube (2013), this model represents local coherence as a graph where nodes are sentences. An edge is drawn if two sentences share at least one common noun. We use the average connectivity of this graph as a proxy for coherence; a sparse graph suggests that the CoT is disjointed or suffers from ‚Äútopic drifting‚ÄĚ due to bias cues.
*   **Lexical Chain**: We identify sequences of semantically related words that span multiple sentences. Each chain represents a ‚Äúsemantic thread‚ÄĚ. We measure the density and continuity of these chains within the biased CoT. Robust reasoning is expected to maintain stable lexical chains, whereas biased reasoning may show fractured or inconsistent terminology.
*   **Repetition Cohesion**: It computes the proportion of occurrences of lexical cohesion devices (primarily word repetitions) within the hypothesis:
    $$RC = \frac{\text{Total occurrences of cohesive devices}}{\text{Total tokens}} \tag{10}$$
    This captures the model‚Äôs tendency to repeat key concepts to maintain focus. While excessive repetition might indicate a ‚Äúlooping‚ÄĚ failure, a sig-

The model outputs a probability distribution across three classes: Positive, Neutral, and Negative. **Semantic Deviation.** We use the all-mpnet-base-v2 Sentence-Transformer (Reimers and Gurevych, 2020) to encode CoT responses into high-dimensional vectors ($v$).

## G Detail Setting in Tuned Lens

The input settings for the tuned lens experiments in Section 6 are presented in Table 9. Our training process for the Tuned Lens follows the procedure of Belrose et al. (2025). The specific training configuration is detailed below.

**Objective.** The training objective is to minimize the difference between the output distribution of the Tuned Lens and the logits distribution of the model‚Äôs final layer. This is essentially a knowledge distillation process, using the final layer output as soft labels.

**Loss Function.** We use Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence as the loss function:

$$\arg \min_{\theta_\ell} \mathbb{E}_x [ D_{KL} (f_{>\ell}(h_\ell) \| \text{TunedLens}_\ell(h_\ell)) ] \tag{12}$$

where $f_{>\ell}(h_\ell)$ denotes all Transformer layers after layer $\ell$ (i.e., the process of generating final logits from the hidden state $h_\ell$ at layer $\ell$), and $\text{TunedLens}_\ell(h_\ell)$ is the output of the Tuned Lens at layer $\ell$ (logits obtained by passing the hidden state $h_\ell$ through an affine transformation followed by the unembedding matrix). This ensures that the predictions of the Tuned Lens do not deviate from the model‚Äôs own final-layer decisions, thereby avoiding learning extraneous irrelevant information.

**Optimizer.** We use the Muon optimizer (Jordan et al., 2024), which maximizes the effective rank of parameter updates through a fast orthogonalization algorithm, addressing the problem of neural network gradient rank deficiency.

**Training Hardware.** All Tuned Lens models are trained on a single node with $8 \times A40$ GPUs.

### I.2 Initial Annotation Instructions

To ensure the consistency of annotation results among the three annotators and minimize the introduction of subjective factors, we formulated the following annotation instructions:

**Objective** The goal of this task is to perform an inductive qualitative analysis to uncover the underlying logic-breaking mechanisms within Chain-of-Thought (CoT) sequences when a model exhibits "sycophancy" (i.e., falsely agreeing with a user‚Äôs incorrect preference or prompt).

## M SAE

### M.1 Sparse Autoencoder (SAEs) Analysis of Sycophancy Dynamics

Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) decompose a model‚Äôs hidden activations into a set of sparse and interpretable latent features, trained via reconstruction objectives with sparsity regularization to isolate semantically meaningful directions (Shu et al., 2025). Compared to linear probes, SAE features provide a distributed yet disentangled representation of internal concepts. While Tuned Lens analyzes final answer preference shifts, SAE feature tracking reveals how sycophancy-aligned semantic structures are progressively strengthened or suppressed across the reasoning process.

### M.2 Experimental Setup

**Semantic Feature Selection.** We identify sycophancy-related SAE features based on their semantic descriptions. Specifically, for each model, we search the Neuronpedia database$^6$ using the keyword *sycophantic*, and select the top 50 SAE features whose explanations exhibit the strongest semantic association with sycophantic behavior. The selected features and the corresponding explanations can be found in Table 14 15 16.

**Models and Layers.** We conduct experiments on Qwen, Gemma, and Llama, focusing on layers where Tuned Lens analysis reveals the earliest and most salient internal preference shifts toward sycophancy or honesty and with an SAE model trained in Bloom et al. (2024). Hence, we analyze SAE features from layer 23 in Qwen, layer 20 in Gemma, and layer 19 in Llama. We do not focus on the final layers because representations in the final layers are often dominated by output-specific mixing and logit projection effects, which makes fine-grained semantic feature dynamics harder to disentangle.

**Data and Trajectory Construction.** For each model, we sample 50 instances from *Type B* where reasoning mitigates sycophancy, and 50 instances from a mixed pool of *Type A* and *Type C*, where reasoning masks or enhances sycophancy.

For each sample, we extract the full CoT generation and divide the token sequence into 100 equal segments, corresponding to normalized reasoning progress from 1% to 100%. For each segment, we extract the hidden states of the tokens falling into the corresponding span, encode each token-level activation using the SAE, and aggregate the resulting feature activations by summation to obtain a segment-level SAE feature representation, which are averaged within each segmentation across samples. Each feature trajectory is normalized independently to highlight its relative activation strength across the reasoning process, without comparing absolute magnitudes between different features.

### M.3 Results and Analysis.

Figure 13 shows the activation trajectories of sycophancy-related SAE features over the normalized reasoning process. Across all examined models and feature sets, we observe a clear and consistent asymmetry between *Type B* and *Type A/C* samples.

*Type B* samples highlight the sycophancy-aligned features typically in early stages, which drop sharply within the first half of the reasoning trajectory and remains suppressed thereafter. This pattern indicates that corrective reasoning in Type B is accompanied by a progressive weakening of sycophancy-related semantic structures, rather than a late-stage override at the output level.

In contrast, *Type A/C* samples show substantially incomplete decay or increase of the same feature set. Sycophancy-related features remain
